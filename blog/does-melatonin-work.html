Does Melatonin Actually Work? What the Science Says

8 min read · Updated June 2026

You've seen the bottles at every pharmacy, drugstore, and Amazon listing. Melatonin gummies, melatonin tablets, melatonin sprays, melatonin patches. It's the #1 sleep supplement in America, and the industry is worth over $1.5 billion.

But here's the question that rarely gets answered clearly: does melatonin actually work?

Not according to marketing copy. Not according to what your friend told you. According to the clinical data.

This article reviews what the research actually says about melatonin — how well it works, who it works for, the risks most articles gloss over, and three alternatives that outperform melatonin for the majority of people with sleep problems.

What Is Melatonin — and How Is It Supposed to Work?

Melatonin is a hormone produced naturally by your pineal gland. Its primary job is to regulate your circadian rhythm — your body's internal clock that tells you when to feel alert and when to feel sleepy.

In a healthy cycle, melatonin production begins rising 1–2 hours before your habitual bedtime, peaks in the middle of the night, and drops off as morning approaches. Light exposure — particularly blue light — suppresses melatonin production, which is why screen time before bed can make it harder to fall asleep.

Melatonin supplements aim to do what your body does naturally: raise blood melatonin levels to signal that it's time to sleep. On paper, the logic is straightforward. In practice, it's more complicated.

Here's the critical distinction most articles miss: melatonin is a timing signal, not a sleep inducer. It tells your body when to sleep — it doesn't force sleep the way a sedative does. This distinction explains both where melatonin excels and where it falls short.

What the Research Actually Says

The evidence for melatonin is more nuanced than supplement companies would like you to believe. Let's separate what the science supports from what it doesn't.

The Conditions Where Melatonin Works Well

Jet lag is where melatonin has the strongest track record. A Cochrane review of 11 randomized controlled trials found that melatonin significantly reduced jet lag symptoms in travelers crossing 5+ time zones, particularly for eastward travel. The effect was consistent and clinically meaningful — not placebo territory.

Delayed Sleep Phase Disorder (DSPD) — a condition where your natural sleep schedule is shifted dramatically later than normal — also responds well to melatonin. Several studies show that low-dose melatonin (0.5 mg) taken 1–2 hours before the desired bedtime helps reset the circadian clock in people with DSPD, including adolescents with chronic delayed sleep onset.

Shift work sleep disorder has moderate support. Melatonin taken before daytime sleep can help night-shift workers improve sleep duration and quality, though the effect is modest compared to environmental interventions like blackout curtains and consistent scheduling.

✅ Strong evidence: Jet lag, Delayed Sleep Phase Disorder
✅ Moderate evidence: Shift work sleep disorder

The Conditions Where Melatonin Is Weak

General insomnia — the most common reason people buy melatonin — has surprisingly modest support. A meta-analysis of 13 trials found that melatonin reduced the time to fall asleep by an average of 7–12 minutes. For comparison, cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) reduces sleep onset time by 30–45 minutes, and the effects are durable after treatment stops.

Why such a small effect? Because most insomnia isn't caused by insufficient melatonin. It's caused by hyperarousal — an overactive nervous system that stays in fight-or-flight mode when it should be in rest-and-digest. Melatonin doesn't address hyperarousal. It's like turning down the thermostat when the real problem is that the heater is stuck on high.

Sleep maintenance — the ability to stay asleep through the night — also shows minimal improvement with melatonin in clinical trials. Melatonin's half-life is only 30–60 minutes, meaning most of it clears your system before you reach the stage of sleep where middle-of-the-night wake-ups occur.

⚠️ Weak evidence: General insomnia (falling asleep)
⚠️ Minimal evidence: Staying asleep through the night

What About the Dosage Problem?

Here's something your melatonin bottle won't tell you: most commercial doses are unnecessarily high.

In the United States, melatonin is regulated as a dietary supplement, not a drug. That means there's no FDA oversight on dosing. Walk into any pharmacy and you'll find 5 mg, 10 mg, even 20 mg tablets. Meanwhile, clinical studies consistently show that 0.3–1 mg is the physiologically effective range for most adults.

Why does this matter? Because supra-physiological doses (anything above 1 mg) can:

  • Cause next-day grogginess — residual melatonin in your system the following morning can leave you feeling foggy and sluggish
  • Disrupt your natural rhythm — flooding your system with excess melatonin can confuse your body's own production cycle, potentially worsening your sleep in the long run
  • Worsen sleep quality — some studies suggest high doses may actually reduce REM sleep and slow-wave (deep) sleep

If you're currently taking 5–10 mg every night, you're taking 5–10 times more than what research shows is effective. And you may be experiencing side effects that you're attributing to something else.

4 Hidden Risks of Melatonin (That Nobody Talks About)

1. It Disrupts Your Natural Production Cycle

Your body's melatonin production is regulated by a feedback loop. When you take an external dose, especially a high one, your brain may reduce its own production to compensate. This is called downregulation, and it's a well-documented phenomenon with hormone supplementation.

The practical consequence: the more you rely on melatonin supplements, the less your body produces on its own. This creates a dependency that has nothing to do with addiction and everything to do with physiology.

2. Long-Term Safety Data Is Missing

Melatonin has been available over-the-counter for decades, but long-term studies — particularly studies tracking users for years or decades — are virtually nonexistent. We know melatonin is safe for short-term use (weeks to months). We do not know what happens after years of daily use.

A 2022 review in Nature and Science of Sleep flagged concerns about melatonin's impact on the immune system, reproductive hormones, and glucose metabolism with prolonged use. The authors emphasized that "long-term safety cannot be assumed based on short-term data."

3. Interactions with Medications

Melatonin can interact with blood thinners (warfarin), diabetes medications, immunosuppressants, and some antidepressants. It's also contraindicated for people with autoimmune conditions because melatonin stimulates the immune response. If you take any prescription medication, you should check for interactions before starting melatonin.

4. Quality Control Is Unreliable

Because melatonin is a supplement, not a drug, what's on the label may not match what's in the bottle. A 2017 study published in the Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine analyzed 31 melatonin supplements and found that 71% did not contain the labeled dosage. One brand contained no melatonin at all — just sugar and filler. Another contained 8.5 mg when the label claimed 1.5 mg.

You have no reliable way to verify what you're actually taking.

What Works Better? 3 Science-Backed Alternatives

If melatonin is a timing signal with modest effects, what addresses the actual root causes of poor sleep? Here are three alternatives with stronger clinical support for general sleep improvement.

1. Magnesium Glycinate — The Muscle Relaxer

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in your body, including the regulation of your nervous system and sleep-wake cycle. Magnesium glycinate — a chelated form of magnesium bound to the amino acid glycine — is the most bioavailable and sleep-specific form available.

How it works: Magnesium supports the production of GABA, your brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. GABA is what turns down the volume on anxious thoughts and racing minds. Low GABA levels are directly linked to insomnia, particularly the "tired but wired" variety.

Glycine, the amino acid attached to the magnesium in this form, also has independent sleep-promoting effects. It lowers core body temperature — a natural trigger for sleep onset — and has been shown in clinical trials to improve subjective sleep quality.

2. L-Theanine — The Calm Without Drowsiness

L-theanine is an amino acid found almost exclusively in green tea. Unlike melatonin, it doesn't affect sleep hormones. Instead, it increases alpha brain wave activity — the same brain wave pattern associated with meditation, deep relaxation, and the state just before falling asleep.

What sets L-theanine apart: it promotes relaxation without causing drowsiness. This makes it particularly useful for people whose sleep problem is a racing mind at night but who need to remain functional during the day.

Clinical studies show that 100–400 mg of L-theanine improves sleep efficiency, reduces sleep latency, and decreases nighttime awakenings. It's also been shown to improve subjective sleep quality in people with ADHD, generalized anxiety disorder, and schizophrenia — suggesting it works on fundamental relaxation pathways rather than through a narrow mechanism like melatonin.

L-theanine is well-tolerated with no known serious side effects and no tolerance buildup. It can be taken daily or as needed without concern for dependency.

3. Sleep Hygiene — The One That Costs Nothing

The most effective "sleep supplement" doesn't come in a bottle. Consistent sleep scheduling — going to bed and waking up at the same time every day, including weekends — has been shown in large-scale trials to improve sleep quality more than any single supplement.

Your circadian rhythm is trained by regularity, not by chemistry. Even the best supplement cannot compensate for an erratic sleep schedule.

Three hygiene factors that matter more than any pill:

  1. Consistent wake-up time — this is the single strongest anchor for your circadian rhythm. Pick a time and stick to it, even on weekends.
  2. Morning light exposure — 10–30 minutes of natural light within an hour of waking signals your pineal gland to stop melatonin production and sets your internal clock for the day.
  3. No screens 30–60 minutes before bed — blue light suppresses melatonin production, but more importantly, content consumption keeps your brain in active problem-solving mode instead of winding down.

When Melatonin Actually Makes Sense

This article isn't anti-melatonin. Melatonin is a legitimate tool — for the right person, at the right dose, in the right situation.

Melatonin makes sense when:

  • You're traveling across 3+ time zones (particularly eastward)
  • You're adjusting to a new night shift schedule
  • You have diagnosed Delayed Sleep Phase Disorder
  • You need temporary adjustment of your sleep schedule for a specific event or deadline

Melatonin does NOT make sense when:

  • You have general insomnia driven by stress or anxiety
  • You wake up in the middle of the night and can't get back to sleep
  • You've been taking it nightly for months without clear benefit
  • You're treating sleep problems that started after age 40 (circadian rhythm changes in older adults may not respond well to supplemental melatonin)

If you fall into any of the "does not make sense" categories, the alternatives above — magnesium glycinate, L-theanine, and improved sleep hygiene — are more likely to address the root cause of your sleep problem.

Bottom Line: Does Melatonin Work?

The honest answer is: it depends on what you're using it for.

If you need to reset your internal clock after traveling or shift work, melatonin works well. The clinical evidence is solid, the dosing protocol is well-established, and the benefits are meaningful.

If you're taking melatonin every night because you have trouble falling asleep or staying asleep — the most common reason people use it — the evidence is underwhelming. Seven to twelve minutes faster sleep onset is not negligible, but it's also not the solution most people are hoping for. And the risks of long-term use, high dosing, and poor quality control are not worth ignoring.

For the average person with general sleep difficulties, magnesium glycinate and L-theanine address the root cause more directly — they calm the nervous system rather than trying to override it with a timing signal. Combined with consistent sleep scheduling and morning light exposure, these approaches produce better, more durable results than melatonin alone.

If you're currently taking melatonin every night and not seeing results, that's not a personal failure — it's a mismatch between the tool and the problem. The right fix depends on understanding what's actually driving your poor sleep.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does melatonin actually work for sleep?

Melatonin works well for circadian rhythm disorders and jet lag, but evidence for general insomnia is mixed. Clinical studies show melatonin reduces sleep onset time by an average of 7–12 minutes — a modest effect compared to other interventions.

What is the correct melatonin dosage?

Most commercial melatonin supplements contain 5–10 mg, but research shows 0.5–1 mg is often more effective. Higher doses can cause next-day grogginess and may disrupt your natural circadian rhythm.

Is it safe to take melatonin every night?

Long-term daily use is not well-studied. Most experts recommend melatonin only for short-term adjustment periods — such as during jet lag or shift work transitions — not as a permanent sleep aid.

What are the side effects of melatonin?

Common side effects include next-day drowsiness, headaches, dizziness, and vivid dreams. The risk increases with higher doses above 5 mg.

What works better than melatonin for sleep?

For most people with general insomnia, magnesium glycinate, L-theanine, and improved sleep hygiene consistently outperform melatonin in clinical studies. Both address the root cause — nervous system overactivation — rather than attempting to override it chemically.

Can melatonin make insomnia worse?

Paradoxically, yes. Taking melatonin at the wrong time or in excessive doses can disrupt your natural circadian rhythm and worsen sleep fragmentation.

Does melatonin help with jet lag?

Yes — jet lag is one of the few conditions where melatonin has strong clinical support. Taking 0.5–5 mg at the target bedtime in your new time zone for 2–3 days after travel can help accelerate circadian re-alignment.

How long does melatonin take to work?

Melatonin typically takes 30–60 minutes to reach peak blood levels. Take it 1–2 hours before your target bedtime. It signals your body to prepare for sleep — it does not induce sleep like a sedative.
